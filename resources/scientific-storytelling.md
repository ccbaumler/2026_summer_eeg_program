# Scientific Storytelling

In recent years there has been a growing cry to improve scientific communication from within the scientific community. Generally, it is hypothesized that by improving our scientific communication we ease the cognitive burden on our audiences. This unburdens colleagues to improve collaborative effort, but, perhaps even more importantly, allows a general audience to partake in our niche domains of science. **Great orators proselytize the essential elements of storytelling as the structure to improve any research talk.**

## Story mapping

A simple framework used frequently by writers is [Freytag's Pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Story_structure). This is a five-part structure used to stage a story:

1. Exposition
2. Rising action
3. Climax
4. Falling action
5. Resolution

## And, But, Therefore

Beth's talk is a detailed exposition of storytelling through story maps (like the hero's journey). For a simpler starting point, the structure PREP scholars at UC Davis are taught is: **And, But, Therefore**. Each represents one key element of storytelling:

- **And**: The exposition. Supply the details required by the audience to understand the remainder of your talk.
- **But**: The climax. Build suspense and intrigue through conflict! Highlight the gaps in our scientific knowledge.
- **Therefore**: The resolution. Provide the audience with closure by stating what you purpose or have done to address this gap in knowledge.

[Further information on the And, But, Therefore method of scientific storytelling can be found here from one of our own PREP scholars at UC Davis.](https://prep.ucdavis.edu/news/science-storytelling)
